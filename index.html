The Problem

Designers have been asked to dream small.

You have a page on the corporate app you are working on. You know it isn't great. You have data showing people don't like it. And now people are looking at you, the designer: We know the page isn't good. Can you help us understand what good looks like?

For a long time, designers have been in a defensive crouch. Beat down by traditional software development, they know that any adjustments they want to make will probably take 18 months. So they shoot lower. Aim smaller. Just to preempt being told their ideas are too expensive.

The problem with that is you end up with watered-down, me-too pages that barely keep pace with competitors — if that. And as the years go on, the software feels more and more creaky and rickety, and it becomes harder to explain why anyone should try your product.

How It Works

Six steps from stale to stellar.

01
Baseline report
Starling analyzes the current screen and tells you why it's having trouble.
02
Divergent exploration
Generate five distinct directions, three screens each, working within your design system.
03
Feedback
Synthetic users weigh in with scores and specific quotes.
04
Feel it move
Click into each variation and feel how it moves.
05
Combine and iterate
Explicitly combine the best elements and do the next round.
06
Converge
Five directions become three, become two, become one. Weak directions culled, strong ones refined.

Why This Matters

The cost of exploration just dropped to nearly zero.

Designers have been asked to dream small for too long. Not because they lack vision, but because the cost of exploring alternatives has been prohibitively high. What if that cost dropped to nearly zero?

That is Starling. Point it at a screen. Walk away. Come back to a full exploration of what good could look like.

It won't replace taste. It won't replace the designer. But it will give you something designers have almost never had: proof that better is possible, and a map of how to get there.
